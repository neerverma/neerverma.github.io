Neighborhood Guides

Travel like a Local

Overview

Traditional and Online guidebooks give people information about those central tourist districts and sights to see. Yelp and Foursquare give people information about the businesses, the bars and restaurants and locksmiths, in an area, but travelers cannot easily understand how the whole neighborhood feels just from that. In order to design tourist guides of the future to help people renting through the sharing economy we developed neighborhood guides from publically available geotagged data and social media data.

Background

An academic research project, where we are creating neighborhood guides from publically available geotagged data and social media data. The primary researcher for the project is Dan Tasse, a PHD student at Carnegie Mellon University. As the designer on the team (advised by Professor Jason Hong), my role was to reimagine the UX and UI for the neighborhood guides .

Final Design Preview




What Travellers want

We started with research, which involved a survey involving 500+ participants and interviewing 17 creative tourists. The key questions we were interested in were:

  • What are the most important factors people consider when they choose a place to stay when they are travelling?
  • What do people wish travelers and movers knew about their neighborhood?
  • What are the different places where people share their opinions and experiences of their travel?

Design Challenges

Even though we have identified the key factors and data sources, here comes the design challenges:

  • How can we make sure that nieghborhood guide is easy to understand?
  • How can we enable the travellers to get to the neighborhoods they might be interested in?
  • How can we enable the travellers to choose between different neighborhoods?

Iterations

I was quickly moving between paper and digital prototyping, testing prototypes with users. From each iteration, I learned something valuable. Some helped me make small usability improvements, some helped me make major changes in my design direction.

Initial iterations were focused on creating a information dashboard

Learning from users

From testing my prototypes with users, I learnt that laying out data is useless unless users have a clear mental model of the data.

People don't get what these Scores and Ratings mean

A lot of people were unsure of what to infer from these ratings. The numbers alone were not enough for users to know the neighborhood.

People don't know which neighborhoods are they interested in

Another interesting finding was that when people are planning their travel, they focus on popular sites rather than an neighborhood in particular.

People still find it hard to choose a neighborhood

Even though we were rating the neighborhood based on certain key factors, a lot of users found that unless they have a big picture of all the neighborhoods, they can't decide on a neighborhood.

Design Decisions


Recommending neighborhoods for each city

Most of the travellers that we tested the prototypes with, were unaware of neighborhoods of the city they are going to.

Rather than Showing individual Score, showing rank for each neighborhood

Even though we were rating the neighborhood based on certain key factors, a lot of users found that unless they have a big picture of all the neighborhoods, they can't decide on a neighborhood.

Showing how individual factor measure up to city's average

I also decided to include how each neighborhood score in that particular factor measure up to city's average to give people an average scale to measure up the factor, they are most interested in.

Showing similar neighborhoods

I decided to show similar neighborhoods in each neighborhood guide page, which is calculated based on the six factors, and it's a easy point for users to explore.
